Childhood lead poisoning is the most important and preventable environmental problem among American children and contributes significantly to the burden of childhood diseases.

Elevated blood lead concentration is associated with lower IQ scores. Students with elevated blood lead concentrations are more inattentive, hyperactive, disorganized, aggressive, and more likely to be delinquent.

Lead exposure in children is often difficult to see. Most children have no obvious immediate symptoms. Exposure to lead can seriously harm a child's health and cause well-documented adverse effects such as:

  • Damage to the brain and nervous system
  • Slowed growth and development
  • Learning and behavior problems
  • Hearing and speech problems

This can cause:
  • Lower IQ
  • Decreased ability to pay attention
  • Underperformance in school

A study in Detroit public schools found that high blood lead levels before 6 years were strongly associated with poor academic achievement in elementary and middle schools. The odds of scoring less than proficient for children whose blood lead levels were greater than 10 micrograms per deciliter were more than twice the odds for those whose blood lead levels were less than 1 microgram per deciliter after adjustment for potential confounders.

In all school subjects studied, children considered advanced had much lower mean blood lead levels than children scoring inadequately on standardized tests.

Another study in Chicago public schools estimated that 13% of reading failures and 14.8% of math failures in children can be attributed to exposure to blood lead concentrations of 5 to 9 µg/dL

Philadelphia has a major childhood blood lead problem, with one out of 25 children tested having elevated levels, compared to one out of 100 children in other cities studied.

Philadelphia children are at high risk throughout the city, but risk is concentrated in some areas due to multiple factors.

Lead-based paint remains the most common source of childhood lead poisoning and is present in millions of older homes. Lead-based paint was banned in new housing construction in 1979, but 84% of Philadelphia county homes were built before the ban.

Additionally, children can be poisoned by lead found in soil. Children in Philadelphia are especially at risk, as Philadelphia was once a major industrial center with large amounts of lead smelters located all over the city.

Even with these smelters no longer operating, the residues they left in the soil remain indefinitely.

These industrial smelters left their impact on Philadelphia. The 500m area of impact around each smelter show how much of the city was affected.

Philadelphia elementary and elementary/middle schools are densely located across the city, many within former smelter impact zones.

Playgrounds in Philadelphia are also affected by lead smelter zones. Many fall within these 500m impact areas.
